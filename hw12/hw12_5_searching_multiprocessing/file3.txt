Stars form in large clouds of gas and dust called molecular clouds. Molecular
clouds range from 1,000 to 10 million times the mass of the Sun and can span
as much as hundreds of light-years. Molecular clouds are cold which causes
gas to clump, creating high-density pockets. Some of these clumps can collide
with each other or collect more matter, strengthening their gravitational
force as their mass grows. Eventually, gravity causes some of these clumps to
collapse. When this happens, friction causes the material to heat up, which
eventually leads to the development of a protostar – a baby star. Batches of
stars that have recently formed from molecular clouds are often called stellar
clusters, and molecular clouds full of stellar clusters are called stellar nurseries.
